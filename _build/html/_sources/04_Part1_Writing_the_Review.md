# Chapter 1.4: Writing the Literature Review

A Literature Review is not a "list". It is a story of the scientific progress in your topic.

## The 5 Essential Writing Templates

Use these sentence structures to sound professional.

### 1. The "Findings-First" Template

*Use when the result is the hero.*
> Author et al. (Year) investigated [topic] and established that [finding], which suggests [implication].

* *Example*: "Smith (2023) investigated coastal heat islands and established that mangroves reduce temperature by 2Â°C, suggesting they are vital for micro-climate regulation."

### 2. The "Methodological Focus" Template

*Use when the tool/algorithm is the hero.*
> Author et al. (Year) employed [method] to analyze [data], demonstrating the efficacy of [tool] for [task].

* *Example*: "Patel (2024) employed Random Forest on Sentinel-2 imagery, demonstrating the efficacy of machine learning for differentiating crop types."

### 3. The "Debate" Template

*Use when contrasting views.*
> While Author A (Year) argued that [Theory X] drives the phenomenon, Author B (Year) contradicted this, emphasizing [Theory Y].

* *Example*: "While Johnson (2010) argued that rainfall drives landslides, Lee (2015) contradicted this, emphasizing that road construction is the primary trigger."

### 4. The "Gap Identification" Template

*Use to justify YOUR study.*
> Author (Year) conducted a study on [topic]; however, the analysis was limited to [limitation], leaving [your topic] largely unexplored.

* *Example*: "Gupta (2020) studied forest fires in India; however, the analysis was limited to the summer season, leaving winter fire dynamics largely unexplored."

### 5. The "Chronological" Template

*Use to show history.*
> Author (Year) expanded upon earlier models by incorporating [new factor], ultimately concluding that [new insight].
