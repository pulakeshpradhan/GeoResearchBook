Part 1: What is Research and Where to Start?#

1. Basics of Research#

Research is not just about “finding information.” It is a systematic process of investigating a specific problem to find new insights.

  • The “Re-” in Research: It implies searching again and again. You look at what others have done, identifying gaps, and trying to fill them.

  • Scientific Method: Observation -> Hypothesis -> Experiment (Analysis) -> Conclusion.

2. Literature Search#

Before you start, you must know what already exists.

  • Sources:

    • Google Scholar: The best starting point.

    • ScienceDirect / Scopus / Web of Science: Premium databases for high-quality journals.

    • ResearchGate: Good for connecting with authors.

  • Keywords: Be specific. Instead of “Flood”, search for “Flash flood monitoring using Sentinel-1 in Urban Areas”.

  • Search Strategy: Use Boolean Operators:

    • "Flood" AND "Remote Sensing" (Results must have both).

    • "Forest Fire" OR "Wildfire" (Results can have either).

3. Literature Analysis (The Excel Strategy)#

Don’t just read PDFs and forget them. Systematize your reading. Based on effective research habits, create an Excel sheet with the following specific columns:

Column

Description

Sl. No.

Serial number to track count.

Authors

Who wrote it? (e.g., Tesfaye et al.)

Title

Full title of the paper.

Journal

Where was it published? (e.g., Environmental Systems Research)

Year

When was it published? (e.g., 2024)

Study Area

Where is the location? (e.g., Ethiopia, Delhi).

Objectives

What did they want to achieve?

Methodology

What tools/algorithms did they use? (e.g., Random Forest, GEE).

Findings

What were the 1-2 key results?

Link

PDF filename or URL to quickly find it again.

4. Literature Review Writing (The Templates)#

When writing your thesis or paper, do not just list the papers. Synthesize them using professional language. Here are 5 Proven Templates you can use to structure your sentences effectively.

Template 1: The “Findings-First” Focus#

Use this when you want to highlight the primary result immediately.

  • Structure: Author et al. (Year) investigated [specific topic] and established that [key finding], which suggests [implication].

  • Example: Smith et al. (2023) investigated the urban heat island effect in coastal cities and established that vegetation cover reduces surface temperatures by up to 3°C, which suggests that green infrastructure is critical for urban cooling strategies.

Template 2: The “Methodological” Focus#

Use this when the tool or algorithm used is the standout feature (common in GEE/GIS).

  • Structure: Author et al. (Year) employed [methodology] to analyze [dataset], demonstrating the efficacy of [tool] in predicting [outcome].

  • Example: Patel et al. (2024) employed Random Forest algorithms to analyze multi-temporal satellite imagery, demonstrating the efficacy of machine learning in predicting rapid land-use changes.

Template 3: The “Debate” or Argument#

Use this when contradicting an old theory or proposing a new one.

  • Structure: Author et al. (Year) argued that [concept] significantly influences [phenomenon], contradicting previous studies which emphasized [opposing factor].

  • Example: Johnson et al. (2022) argued that socio-economic status significantly influences flood vulnerability, contradicting previous studies which emphasized physical elevation as the sole determinant.

Template 4: The “Gap Identification”#

Use this to set the stage for YOUR research by showing what is missing.

  • Structure: Author et al. (Year) conducted a comprehensive study on [topic]; however, their analysis was limited to [limitation], leaving the impact of [your focus] largely unexplored.

  • Example: Lee et al. (2021) conducted a comprehensive study on groundwater depletion; however, their analysis was limited to arid regions, leaving the impact of depletion in tropical monsoon climates largely unexplored.

Template 5: The “Chronological” Evolution#

Use this to show how science has improved over time.

  • Structure: Author et al. (Year) expanded upon earlier models of [concept] by incorporating [new variable], ultimately concluding that [refined conclusion].

  • Example: Gupta et al. (2023) expanded upon earlier models of crop yield estimation by incorporating Sentinel-1 SAR data, ultimately concluding that radar data significantly improves accuracy during cloud-cover periods.
