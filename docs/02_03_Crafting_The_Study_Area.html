Chapter 2.3: Crafting the Study Area#

In Geography, context is everything. A flood in a desert is different from a flood in a delta.

1. Why describe the Study Area?#

It tells the reader about the physical and social constraints of your experiment.

  • “The area usually receives 2000mm of rain” -> This explains why a flood is expected.

  • “The population density is 20,000 per sq/km” -> This explains why the impact is high.

2. Essential Components#

  1. Geography: Coordinates (Lat/Long), Area (in sq km).

  2. Topography: Elevation range (DEM), Slope. (Floods happen in lowlands; landslides on slopes).

  3. Climate: Annual Rainfall, Temperature extremities, Seasons.

  4. Demographics: Population, Urban/Rural split.

  5. Land Use: Is it mostly agricultural? Urban? Forest?

3. The Study Area Map (Cartography 101)#

A map is mandatory. It must be professional.

  • Hierarchy:

    • Inset 1: Where is India in the World? (Optional).

    • Inset 2: Where is the State in India?

    • Main Map: The District/City boundary.

  • Elements:

    • North Arrow: Always pointing Up.

    • Scale Bar: In Metric (km), not Imperial (miles).

    • Legend: Clearly label “River”, “Urban Area”, “Boundary”.

    • Grid: Lat/Long text on the borders.

  • Source Credit: “Data Source: Census 2011; Map generated by Author.”

4. Writing the Description#

“The study focuses on the Cuttack district of Odisha, located between 20°N and 21°N latitude. Geographically, it is part of the Mahanadi Delta, characterized by flat alluvial terrain with an average elevation of 15m above Mean Sea Level (MSL). The climate is tropical monsoon, receiving 1500mm of rainfall annually, 80% of which occurs during the Southwest Monsoon (June-September).”
